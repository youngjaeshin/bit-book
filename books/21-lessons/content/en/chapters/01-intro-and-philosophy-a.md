# Intro + Philosophy A

## Chapter Digest

Bitcoin is introduced as a rabbit hole rather than a product. Gigi frames the book as an initiation into a way of seeing that unsettles ordinary assumptions about money, technology, and the self. The foreword and introduction insist that Bitcoin cannot be reduced to protocol diagrams or price charts; it sits at the intersection of philosophy, economics, and technology, and each lens reveals something the others miss. The first move is deliberate disorientation: Bitcoin teaches how much of the surrounding monetary world had been taken for granted.

That philosophical shock begins with immutability and scarcity. Bitcoin is not ordinary software that can be rewritten whenever enough users want a change. It is a decentralized social system, so altering it means persuading a dispersed network to accept the change. Scarcity matters for the same reason. Unlike digital files that can be copied without limit, Bitcoin combines open verification and replication with a fixed monetary boundary, producing a digital asset that preserves reproducibility while refusing inflationary abundance.

The introduction does more than frame the book. It sets the central claim in motion: Bitcoin changes the person trying to understand it. Time, scarcity, and the limits of control become unavoidable once the rabbit hole opens, and the rest of the book follows the consequences of that realization across philosophy, economics, and technology.
