# What Is Bitcoin Good For?

## Chapter Digest

Bitcoin is most useful where the existing monetary order fails hardest. It offers a store of value outside the direct reach of central-bank dilution, a payment network that can move across borders without relying on correspondent banking, and a savings technology for people living under capital controls, chronic inflation, or politically fragile financial systems. Its value is therefore not exhausted by day-to-day retail payments; it begins with monetary escape from systems that quietly confiscate wealth.

That usefulness becomes clearest in places where sovereignty over money matters more than convenience. Someone in a stable banking jurisdiction may treat custody risk, censorship risk, and monetary debasement as distant abstractions. Someone in a collapsing currency regime, by contrast, experiences them directly. In that environment, the ability to hold value in an asset with fixed issuance and to transmit it without asking permission can be the difference between preserving savings and watching them disappear.

Bitcoin is also useful because it separates settlement from trust in institutions. The owner can verify supply, validate transactions, and hold funds without delegating final control to a bank. That does not eliminate operational risk—self-custody demands competence—but it does shift the nature of the risk away from opaque institutions and toward transparent personal responsibility. For Ammous, that trade is one of Bitcoin’s defining strengths rather than a drawback.

At the same time, the chapter resists turning Bitcoin into a cure for every problem. It is not a better database for every use case, nor a perfect substitute for all existing financial infrastructure. Its importance lies in solving a specific and historically rare problem: creating scarce digital money that remains outside discretionary political control. Once that problem is solved, other functions can be built around it, but the monetary breakthrough comes first.

The practical answer to the chapter’s title is therefore layered. Bitcoin is good for storing value across time, for moving value across hostile political boundaries, for limiting the hidden tax of inflation, and for restoring a form of financial self-command that fiat systems steadily eroded. Those uses may not matter equally to everyone at every moment, but together they explain why Bitcoin can be dismissed as unnecessary in tranquil times and then become indispensable when the monetary order itself turns unstable.
