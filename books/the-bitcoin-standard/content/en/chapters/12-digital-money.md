# Digital Money

## Chapter Digest

Digital money had long seemed impossible because digital objects are easy to copy. Any token that can be reproduced without cost cannot function as cash unless a trusted intermediary keeps the ledger and prevents double-spending. Before Bitcoin, that meant every successful digital payment system remained dependent on banks, card networks, or platform operators. Cash retained finality and independence only in physical form, while every electronic transfer required permission, supervision, and delayed settlement through third parties.

Bitcoin changes that by making a digital object scarce and independently verifiable. Through a distributed network, digital signatures, hashing, proof-of-work, and difficulty adjustment, it allows strangers to agree on who owns what without granting a central authority the power to edit balances at will. The novelty is not simply that payments move online; it is that final settlement can now happen digitally without asking a bank, processor, or government-backed gatekeeper to keep the books honest.

That makes Bitcoin the first credible form of digital cash. It inherits the strongest features of physical cash—direct transfer, finality, and reduced dependence on third parties—while improving on its salability across space. Someone can move value globally without carrying metal or banknotes, and the system’s monetary policy remains shielded from discretionary inflation because the issuance schedule is enforced by the protocol rather than by a monetary committee.

The larger point is that money evolves with technology, and each improvement changes the balance between convenience, soundness, and sovereignty. Metal coinage improved on irregular lumps of metal; gold-backed banking improved on direct movement of specie while introducing new vulnerabilities; government money improved scale while destroying monetary discipline. Bitcoin is presented as the next such step: a digital monetary technology that restores hard constraints and owner control in a world where both had been steadily eroded.
