# Bitcoin Questions II

## Chapter Digest

The later objections turn from technical novelty to institutional replacement. If Bitcoin grows, what happens to banks, central banks, and the political uses of money that have defined the modern era? Ammous argues that many fears about Bitcoin stem from assuming the present fiat architecture is natural and indispensable. Once money itself becomes hard and politically difficult to expand, banking, credit, and state finance do not disappear, but they lose the ability to hide their costs through inflation and monetary privilege.

That is why Bitcoin is contrasted with the broader blockchain fashion of the period. Most alternative crypto projects are treated as attempts to graft speculative novelty onto problems that do not require decentralized money. Bitcoin stands apart because it solves a specific monetary problem and accepts the costs necessary to solve it. Its simplicity and conservatism are therefore strengths: they keep the system focused on hard money rather than diffusing it into endless experimentation.

It also returns to the social consequences of monetary discipline. A system that cannot be easily inflated changes the incentives faced by governments, financial institutions, and ordinary savers. It makes war, bailouts, and deficit spending harder to disguise, while making long-term saving and capital preservation easier to understand. In that sense, the question is no longer whether Bitcoin has “use,” but whether societies are willing to live with a money that is harder to bend toward short-term political goals.

Its closing force comes from this inversion. Bitcoin is not judged against an idealized fiat order that never existed; fiat is judged against the possibility of money that can no longer be quietly manipulated. Once that possibility is taken seriously, many standard objections look less like decisive refutations and more like defenses of institutions that benefited from monetary opacity. Bitcoin’s challenge is therefore broader than payments or investment. It is a challenge to the monetary habits of the modern state.
