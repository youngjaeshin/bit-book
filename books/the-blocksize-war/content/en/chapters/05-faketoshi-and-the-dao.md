Gavin Andresen's Craig Wright announcement lands in May 2016 like a second legitimacy shock. The claim that Craig Steven Wright is Satoshi Nakamoto does not resolve the blocksize war; it instead pulls the community back into proof, doubt, and personality politics. Roger Ver and others are eager to see Wright as a usable ally, but the episode mostly shows how brittle Bitcoin hero worship becomes when identity can be staged and weaponized.

The claim is important less for who Wright is than for what the community does around the claim. Verification, trust, and reputation all get folded into an already poisonous argument over who speaks for Bitcoin. A supposed founder can be turned into a political instrument, and that tells you something uncomfortable about how much of the struggle has become about authority rather than software.

The DAO hack in Ethereum gives Bitcoiners a live example of the governance nightmare they fear. When the hack is reversed through a contentious hardfork and the chain splits into Ethereum and Ethereum Classic, the lesson is hard to miss: if emergency intervention becomes normal, history can be rewritten whenever a visible inner circle decides that necessity is enough. Even if the technical details differ, the social lesson cuts straight across the blocksize debate. The whole episode showed how easily a founder claim can be turned into a factional weapon.
