August 15, 2015 lands in the story as a clean rupture. Mike Hearn and Gavin Andresen release and endorse Bitcoin XT, turning a technical dispute into an open challenge to Bitcoin’s existing social contract. Bitcoin XT is presented as a path to 8 MB blocks and then an ongoing doubling schedule, because the 1 MB limit is already close to binding and full blocks threaten the dream of cheap global payments. Opponents hear something very different: a hardfork client that could split the network, change the rules without broad consent, and make every validating node choose sides. Even before anyone is talking about doctrine, the first question is whether Bitcoin can tolerate a contentious change at all.

That first move exposes the deeper fault lines that keep returning throughout the war. Blockspace scarcity, upgrade procedure, the role of ordinary validating users, and time preference all sit underneath the headline fight over block size. The support letter from major companies and the later, softer BIP 103 proposal show how quickly the discussion moves from a single number to competing ideas of legitimacy. A moderate path still fails because the large-block camp wants decisive expansion and the small-block camp will not accept a rushed hardfork. What begins as an apparent product decision becomes a struggle over who gets to define Bitcoin, and how much force is acceptable when trying to do it.
