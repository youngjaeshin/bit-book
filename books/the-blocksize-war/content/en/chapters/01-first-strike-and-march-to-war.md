August 15, 2015 lands like a rupture. Mike Hearn and Gavin Andresen release and endorse Bitcoin XT, and the fight instantly stops being a narrow technical disagreement about block limits. XT proposes 8 MB blocks and a continuing doubling schedule, because the 1 MB cap is already close to binding and the payment-network promise of Bitcoin looks cramped by congestion. Opponents hear something else entirely: a hardfork client that could split the network, alter the rules without broad consent, and force every validating node to choose sides.

The argument underneath the headline is about Bitcoin's social contract. A system that began with one client, one codebase, and a small set of users now has to decide who gets to change the rules, how quickly they may change, and whether ordinary validators can be pushed around by urgency. Blockspace scarcity, time preference, and the role of users sit beneath the surface of the blocksize number. Once the debate is framed that way, the question is no longer whether 1 MB is too small; it is whether Bitcoin can survive a contentious upgrade at all.

Support letters from major companies and later compromise proposals such as BIP 103 show how fast the fight moves from engineering to legitimacy. The large-block camp wants decisive expansion, while the small-block camp will not accept a rushed hardfork just because a few prominent figures want one. Every attempt at a moderate path keeps running into the same wall: a change that feels practical to one side looks like coercion to the other.

What starts like a product decision becomes a contest over who can define Bitcoin. The first strike makes the deeper war visible, and the rest of the conflict keeps circling back to that question of authority.
