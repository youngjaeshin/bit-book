The New York Agreement is the last major attempt to stitch the coalition together from above. Barry Silbert gathers miners, companies, and prominent figures in New York and presents a package: SegWit first, then a 2 MB hardfork within six months. The signatories are supposed to look like a critical mass of the ecosystem, and the document is written that way, with an 80 percent SegWit threshold and activation signaled at bit 4.

The problem is that the agreement treats a protocol change as if it can be managed through elite coordination, even after years of arguments that users are the final authority. Bit 4 confusion and the need for BIP91 show how fragile the activation mechanics really are, and the promised second phase has no obvious legitimacy once people start asking about replay protection and the consequences of a split.

The agreement therefore becomes a stress test rather than a solution. It shows that a coalition built around signatures and private meetings can push SegWit into place, but it cannot make the network accept a larger hardfork by fiat. The more the details come into focus, the more the deal looks like a paper majority that has not earned the right to speak for Bitcoin.
