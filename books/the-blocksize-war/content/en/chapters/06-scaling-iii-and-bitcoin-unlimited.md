Scaling III in Milan feels like the war has changed shape. Ethereum's excitement has cooled, and small blockers use the conference to move on from the blocksize deadlock toward Lightning, Schnorr signatures, and other upgrades that do not require a contested hardfork. The event is dominated by the small-block side, and the old showdown atmosphere is mostly gone, even though a smaller large-block contingent is still present.

That contingent is no longer just a loose set of advocates. Bitcoin Unlimited becomes the new vehicle for the large-block camp, with membership, by-laws, voting, and a philosophy that miners and users should choose blocksize parameters for themselves. The idea sounds flexible, but the technical reality is messier: sticky gates, median-EB attacks, xThin surprises, and a stubborn belief that miners will behave well enough to make the design safe all pile up behind the rhetoric.

Milan makes the social balance of power obvious. Jihan Wu is frustrated by the lack of hardfork discussion and withdraws SegWit signaling, while the conference itself looks less like a crisis summit and more like a technical gathering where the small-block camp has already moved on. The war has not ended; it has shifted from argument about a single number into a slower contest of organization, credibility, and implementation quality.
