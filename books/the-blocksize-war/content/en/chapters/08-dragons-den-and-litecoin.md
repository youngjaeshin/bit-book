The Dragons' Den scandal pushes the war deeper into information politics. When Joseph Poon mentions a secret small-block channel used to organize PR and trolling, the large-block side quickly discovers the dragonsden Slack room and concludes that both camps are running backstage campaigns. Memes, leaks, and private coordination become part of the struggle over legitimacy, not a side show around it.

That atmosphere spills into the next round of signaling battles. Bram Cohen's earlier presentation, and the suspicion that the small-block camp has been building its narrative in private for a long time, makes the social contest feel even more deliberate. The public fight is still about code, but it is now impossible to separate code from messaging, coalition management, and the politics of who gets to look honest.

Litecoin becomes the proving ground. SegWit activation there is used to show that the upgrade works and to embarrass the large-block warnings, yet the path is messy: miner signaling stalls, users push for a Litecoin UASF, Chinese miners respond with more hashpower and a roundtable agreement, and the final result is a small, contradictory settlement that still sends a strong message back to Bitcoin. By the time SegWit activates on Litecoin, the small-block camp has a live example of pressure and coordination moving miners. Litecoin became the rehearsal that proved coordination pressure could move miners in the real world.
