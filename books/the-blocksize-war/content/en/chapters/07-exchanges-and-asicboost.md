Exchanges become decisive once they stop behaving like neutral plumbing. Businesses such as Poloniex, BitMEX, Bitfinex, Bitstamp, and Coinbase are now shaping price formation and can be lobbied by both sides of the war. The larger market in the Asia-Pacific region gives these firms real leverage, and the small-block camp generally does a better job persuading them because its argument looks more pragmatic and less tied to the old blocksize slogans.

That makes the exchange layer part of the conflict itself. Joint statements, support declarations, and later futures markets for possible chain splits turn protocol disagreement into something traders can hedge and price. Liquidity providers are no longer just watching the war from the sidelines; they are deciding which chain will be treated as Bitcoin when the market has to choose.

Gregory Maxwell's ASICBoost accusation shifts the tone again. If Bitmain is resisting SegWit because a mining shortcut would be threatened by the new format, then the blocksize fight is not just about throughput or philosophy. It may also be about hidden mining advantage. Once exchange behavior and mining incentives are read as strategic positions rather than neutral opinions, the conflict starts to look like a contest over who can best disguise self-interest. That is what made market structure part of the protocol war.
