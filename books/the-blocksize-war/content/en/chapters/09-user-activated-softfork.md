Shaolinfry's UASF idea turns the Litecoin lesson into a direct threat to Bitcoin's miners. In the February 25, 2017 mailing-list email, the argument is simple: supermajority hash-power signaling gives miners too much political attention and lets a single lagging miner veto a softfork that users already want. The alternative is flag-day activation, where nodes begin enforcing the rules on a predetermined date with enough lead time for the network to prepare.

Gregory Maxwell's opposition shows why the proposal is so divisive even inside the small-block camp. SegWit had been designed to minimize disruption, and forcing a deadline onto miners introduces risks that the original activation path tried to avoid. The objections are serious, but the proposal still does its political work by making user enforcement concrete instead of theoretical.

UASF changes the balance of power in the story. It says validating users are not spectators waiting for miner consent; they can force the network to defend its own rules. That threat becomes leverage, and the mere existence of the idea helps move the conflict toward a world where miners have to answer to the base of the network rather than the other way around.
