SegWit2x is the last serious attempt to force a compromise, and it collapses under the weight of everything the war has taught both sides. The campaign against phase two stays quiet until SegWit is locked in, but once August 2017 arrives the small-block side has a clear user majority and enough trader support to turn the pressure back on. The story has already gone through several rounds: Gavin Andresen and Mike Hearn, then Roger Ver and Jihan Wu, and now Jeff Garzik and Mike Belshe are the faces left to defend the final compromise.

The coalition starts to fall apart under market pressure. Exchanges label the coin B2X, businesses defect, F2Pool pulls back, BitMEX refuses to support a hardfork without default replay protection, and even parts of the large-block camp drift toward Bitcoin Cash instead of the SegWit2x compromise. By the time the activation date gets close, the supposed majority looks much smaller than it did on paper.

On November 8, Mike Belshe and the other supporters withdraw the proposal. The explanation is as political as it is technical: there is no user consensus, no reliable node network, and no path to a split that would not just create a weaker altcoin. When BTC1 reaches its activation point, bugs stop it from producing a chain at all. The war ends with the small-block camp proving more effective at persuasion, coordination, and legitimacy, and Bitcoin comes out with a stronger norm that rule changes must pass through users, not just hashpower or corporate signatures.
