SegWit2x is the last serious attempt to force a compromise, and it collapses under the weight of everything the war has taught both sides. The plan starts with the New York Agreement, then rides SegWit’s activation, but by autumn 2017 the support base is evaporating. Exchanges start labeling the split coin as B2X, major businesses defect, F2Pool backs away, BitMEX refuses to support the fork without default replay protection, and the original chain steadily accumulates social and economic legitimacy. Even the large-block side is drifting toward Bitcoin Cash rather than toward the compromise it signed.

On November 8, Mike Belshe and the other supporters withdraw the proposal, and the reasons are as political as they are technical: the project has no user consensus, no reliable node network, and no path to a split that would not simply create a weaker altcoin. When BTC1 finally reaches its activation point, critical bugs stop it from producing a chain at all. That failure closes the war for good. The small-block camp does not win because it is loudest; it wins because it proves more effective at persuasion, coordination, and legitimacy, leaving Bitcoin with a stronger norm that rule changes must pass through users, not just hashpower or corporate signatures.
