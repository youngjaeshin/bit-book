# THE FEDERAL RESERVE

## Chapter Digest

The Federal Reserve is presented as the institution with the singular power to create money out of thin air, and therefore as the central engine of the current system. Its formal mandates and public language suggest stewardship and stability, but its actual behavior is described as discretionary, opaque, and repeatedly tilted toward protecting the financial order rather than imposing discipline on it.

Episodes such as liquidity crises, bank rescues, and emergency interventions are used to show how the Fed’s power expands precisely when prior distortions begin to break down. Instead of functioning as a neutral guardian, it becomes the institution that postpones correction, socializes risk, and entrenches moral hazard. The result is a system in which private gains and public costs are repeatedly fused through monetary authority.

## Key Takeaways

- The Fed’s unique power is the ability to create money at discretion.
- Official mandates do not match the chapter’s description of actual behavior.
- Crisis response repeatedly expands Fed power.
- Intervention delays correction and deepens moral hazard.
- The Fed is treated as a core pillar of the inflationary system.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Money creation "out of thin air" is the defining institutional power.
- Emergency intervention becomes a pattern, not an exception.
- The Fed protects the financial order more than it disciplines it.
