# CLASSICAL ECONOMICS

## Chapter Digest

Classical economics is introduced as the tradition that understood production, saving, capital formation, and market coordination before modern macroeconomic management displaced those priorities. Wealth grows through discipline, deferred consumption, and the accumulation of capital, not through artificial stimulation. Interest rates, in this framework, emerge from time preference and the real relationship between saving and investment.

The chapter presents this tradition as both descriptively accurate and morally serious. It respects cause and effect, treats prices and interest rates as signals rather than policy levers, and links prosperity to productive behavior rather than monetary manipulation. The point is not historical nostalgia, but the recovery of an economic framework that still explains how real growth occurs.

## Key Takeaways

- Classical economics centers saving, production, and capital formation.
- Interest rates are tied to time preference and real market conditions.
- Prosperity comes from disciplined accumulation, not artificial demand stimulus.
- Prices and rates are signals that coordinate economic activity.
- The chapter positions classical economics as a better guide than modern interventionist frameworks.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Time preference is central to understanding interest.
- Capital formation is treated as the basis of real growth.
- Market signals are distorted when policy overrides them.
