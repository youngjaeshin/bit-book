# THE PATH TO PURE FIAT: 1789-1971

## Chapter Digest

The American monetary story is presented as a long struggle between the public and banking power. From the founding era onward, debates over central banking, convertibility, and monetary restraint are treated as recurring fights over who controls money and who benefits from that control. Each institutional change is shown as a step away from external discipline and toward discretionary management.

By the time the gold link is finally severed, the path to pure fiat has already been prepared through war finance, depression management, and repeated promises that expanded monetary power would stabilize the system. The chapter treats those promises as failures. What remains by 1971 is a monetary order increasingly detached from restraint and increasingly vulnerable to abuse.

## Key Takeaways

- American monetary history is framed as a conflict between public interest and banking power.
- Institutional changes steadily weakened convertibility and external restraint.
- Wars, crises, and emergency measures repeatedly expanded discretionary monetary control.
- Promises of stability justified each step toward fiat.
- By 1971, the system had been prepared for a fully unconstrained fiat regime.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Central banking is treated as a contested political project, not a neutral administrative necessity.
- Convertibility functioned as a form of restraint.
- Pure fiat is the result of a long cumulative process, not a sudden break alone.
