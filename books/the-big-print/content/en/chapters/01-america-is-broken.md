# AMERICA IS BROKEN

## Chapter Digest

The American financial system is not merely imperfect; it is structurally dependent on inflation. Inflation is not simply a matter of rising prices, but a mechanism that channels wealth upward. People who own assets and have ready access to credit and capital benefit from that structure, while ordinary workers and savers bear the cost.

Record wealth inequality is the clearest visible symptom of that system. The top 1 percent have captured an extraordinary share of national wealth, while the bottom half have made little progress. Technology, offshoring, and productivity changes contributed to the gap, but asset inflation driven by mispriced capital has been the more powerful cause. Inequality is therefore not an accidental outcome, but a predictable consequence of the monetary order.

The same logic extends into politics and social life. Falling trust in government is another symptom of systemic failure: if overwhelming public preferences rarely translate into action, then the governing structure no longer operates credibly on behalf of the public. Distrust, populist anger, and institutional dysfunction are consequences of a system that rewards insiders and distances power from ordinary citizens.

The diagnosis does not stop at finance. Poor health outcomes, medical bankruptcy, deindustrialization, soaring executive pay, and deaths of despair all belong to the same explanation. These are not separate policy failures but connected expressions of a deeper disorder in the monetary and financial system.

## Key Takeaways

- The chapter frames inflation as a structural wealth-transfer mechanism, not just a story about prices rising.
- Asset owners and those with access to capital benefit disproportionately from the current system.
- Record wealth inequality and collapsing trust in government are presented as linked outcomes of financial distortion.
- Social decay — from medical bankruptcy to deaths of despair — is treated as part of the same systemic breakdown.
- The chapter sets up the book’s larger argument that America’s problems are rooted in the monetary order itself.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Inflation is presented as more than "prices going up"; it is tied to asset inflation and unequal wealth distribution.
- Mispriced capital is a central explanatory idea in the chapter.
- The chapter uses wealth inequality, political distrust, and health/social decline as parallel evidence that "the system is broken."
