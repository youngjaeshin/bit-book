# KEYNESIANS AND MODERN MONETARY THEORY (MMT)

## Chapter Digest

Competing schools of economic thought are compared here in order to show that policy is always downstream from ideas. Classical and Austrian traditions are presented as respecting limits, production, savings, and market coordination, while Keynesianism and MMT are described as intellectual frameworks that justify activist management, monetary expansion, and persistent state intervention.

The dispute is not treated as academic. Once policy is built on theories that normalize deficits, demand management, and monetary elasticity, inflation and financial distortion become features rather than accidents. The chapter therefore frames the battle of schools as a battle over whether the economy is governed by real constraints or by theories that claim those constraints can be engineered away.

## Key Takeaways

- Economic policy follows underlying theory.
- Classical and Austrian frameworks are aligned with limits, saving, and market signals.
- Keynesianism and MMT are presented as justifications for intervention and monetary expansion.
- Inflationary policy is linked to bad theory, not merely bad execution.
- The chapter treats the contest of ideas as politically decisive.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- MMT is treated as a late and extreme form of fiat thinking.
- Austrian economics is positioned as the clearest intellectual alternative.
- The debate concerns real constraints versus managerial illusion.
