# EMBRACE DEFLATION

## Chapter Digest

Deflation is defended here against one of the deepest taboos in mainstream economics. Falling prices are not inherently destructive; under conditions of productivity growth, they can be the natural expression of abundance, efficiency, and technological progress. The standard fear of deflation is treated as a category error that confuses healthy price declines with collapse caused by prior credit excess.

The chapter argues that a sound system should allow people to benefit from progress through lower prices and stronger purchasing power. To resist deflation at all costs is therefore to defend artificial scarcity and to privilege debt structures over consumers, savers, and honest production. Deflation, in the right context, is a sign of economic health rather than disease.

## Key Takeaways

- Deflation is not inherently bad.
- Productivity-driven price declines can reflect abundance and progress.
- Mainstream fear of deflation confuses healthy adjustment with credit collapse.
- Preventing deflation protects debt structures at the expense of savers and consumers.
- A sound monetary order should allow purchasing power to rise over time.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Deflation is reframed as a benefit under healthy conditions.
- Central-bank fear of deflation is treated as conceptual error.
- Rising purchasing power is presented as normal and desirable.
