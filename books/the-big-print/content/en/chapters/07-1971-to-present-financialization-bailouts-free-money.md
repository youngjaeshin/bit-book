# 1971 TO PRESENT: FINANCIALIZATION, BAILOUTS, FREE MONEY

## Chapter Digest

After 1971, the move away from sound money accelerates into a regime of financialization, serial bailouts, and progressively cheaper money. Credit expands, the financial sector grows in relative weight, and the rules increasingly protect insiders from failure while leaving ordinary citizens exposed to the consequences. The system becomes more dependent on intervention precisely because earlier interventions created fragility.

The chapter reads the post-1971 era as a sequence of escalating distortions: boom, crisis, rescue, and renewed excess. Zero rates, quantitative easing, and repeated support for financial institutions do not solve the underlying problem; they deepen dependence on monetary manipulation. What appears as stabilization is treated instead as a more advanced stage of addiction.

## Key Takeaways

- The post-1971 era is defined by growing financialization and monetary intervention.
- Bailouts protect insiders while socializing losses.
- Cheap money encourages further leverage and fragility.
- Each rescue lays the groundwork for the next crisis.
- Stabilization policy is reframed as a deepening addiction to intervention.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Financialization is treated as a symptom of fiat excess.
- Bailouts create a two-tiered system.
- ZIRP and QE are described as accelerants of distortion rather than cures.
