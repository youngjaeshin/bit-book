# Everyone Feels the Problem

## Chapter Digest

The chapter begins from the ordinary experience of prices rising everywhere while wages fail to keep up. Housing, food, tuition, healthcare, and daily necessities all demand more money over time, yet this persistent deterioration is often treated as normal. That normalization is itself part of the problem. Inflation is not framed as a harmless background condition but as a force that slowly destroys the economic promises people were told to trust, especially the promise that diligent work and restraint would lead to stability.

From there the argument shifts from personal frustration to structural diagnosis. The growing gap between ordinary households and the wealthy is not explained as a sudden outbreak of laziness below or greed above. The deeper cause is broken money that systematically transfers purchasing power upward and makes social mobility harder with each passing year. Official inflation measures further obscure the damage by understating how sharply the cost of real life has climbed, especially in categories like housing and family formation.

The chapter then makes inflation concrete through dilution. New money creation lowers the purchasing power of existing money just as increasing the supply of a scarce collectible destroys its premium. Because new money does not enter the economy evenly, those closest to the source receive its benefits first while everyone else absorbs the higher prices later. The result is a world in which people follow every respectable rule and still feel themselves sinking. Inflation is not merely higher prices; it is a system that turns effort into stagnation and stagnation into self-blame.

## Key Takeaways

- Rising prices are treated as a monetary problem, not as an unavoidable fact of life.
- Inflation is presented as a force that breaks the promise of upward mobility.
- Wealth concentration is tied to monetary structure rather than to simple moral stories.
- Official measures can hide how much living costs have really risen.
- New money creation dilutes purchasing power unevenly across society.
