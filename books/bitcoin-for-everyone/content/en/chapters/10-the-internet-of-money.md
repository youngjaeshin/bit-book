# The Internet of Money

## Chapter Digest

Bitcoin is cast here as a transformation comparable to the internet itself. Just as the internet allowed information to move across the world without centralized gatekeepers, Bitcoin allows value to move across a global network without depending on banks, states, or legacy payment rails to grant permission. The comparison is not rhetorical flourish alone; it is meant to show that the significance of Bitcoin lies in changing the architecture of exchange, not merely in offering one more financial product.

The chapter stresses that network effects are central. A communication network becomes more valuable as more people join it, and a monetary network can behave similarly. The more widely Bitcoin is adopted, the stronger, more useful, and more resilient it becomes. That is why its significance cannot be measured only at the level of present-day usage. Like earlier foundational technologies, it may be misunderstood precisely because its broader implications emerge only as the network grows.

The internet analogy also highlights the human dimension of decentralization. Middlemen are not removed only to reduce cost; they are removed to restore directness, permissionlessness, and equal access. Bitcoin is therefore presented as the internet of money because it lets value travel with the same openness that the internet brought to information.

## Key Takeaways

- Bitcoin is compared to the internet as a foundational network transformation.
- Its significance lies in changing how value moves, not just in adding another asset.
- Network effects are central to Bitcoin’s growth and resilience.
- Decentralization restores direct access and weakens gatekeepers.
- Bitcoin is framed as an open value network analogous to the internet.
