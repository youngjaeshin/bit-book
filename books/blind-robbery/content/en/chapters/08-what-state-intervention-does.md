# What Happens When the State Intervenes in Everything

## Chapter Digest

The chapter extends the critique of bad money into a broader critique of interventionism. Monetary distortion is not isolated from regulation, welfare programs, subsidies, or administrative rule-making; it helps sustain and amplify them. Once the state can expand money and credit, it gains room to intervene in far more areas of life than direct taxation alone would politically permit.

Intervention then creates secondary distortions that are used to justify further intervention. Prices, incentives, and institutions stop reflecting voluntary coordination and begin reflecting layered political management. This does not produce stability but cumulative complexity. Each intervention obscures the effects of earlier ones and creates new demands for correction from the same authority that caused the distortion in the first place.

The chapter’s larger claim is that bad money and interventionism are mutually reinforcing. Monetary expansion finances intervention, and intervention creates conditions that appear to require still more monetary and political management. The result is not competent stewardship but a self-thickening web of dependence and distortion.

## Key Takeaways

- Monetary expansion gives the state room to widen intervention.
- One intervention tends to generate secondary problems and further interventions.
- Political management replaces voluntary coordination across more of society.
- Complexity grows while transparency declines.
- Bad money and interventionism reinforce each other.
