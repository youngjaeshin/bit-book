# Why Government Money Ruins Us Economically

## Chapter Digest

Government money ruins the economy because credit expansion suppresses the information that interest rates are supposed to convey. In a healthy system, rates reflect the tradeoff between present consumption and future saving. When the state and banking system force rates below their natural level, entrepreneurs are misled into believing that society has set aside more real resources for long production processes than it actually has. Projects that look profitable on paper then begin to consume capital that was never truly available.

The familiar boom that follows is therefore unstable from the start. New construction, speculative asset prices, and rapid lending can all rise together even while the underlying pool of savings is being stretched thin. The apparent prosperity is built on malinvestment: resources are directed into lines of production that would not have been chosen if prices had reflected real scarcity. Once consumers, workers, and firms confront reality, those projects cannot all be completed, and the economy is forced into a painful correction.

The bust is not a mysterious failure of capitalism. It is the liquidation phase of an artificial boom. Bad investments have to be written down, labor and materials have to be reallocated, and the capital stock has to be rebuilt on a sounder basis. The reason the collapse feels so abrupt is that the distortion lasted so long. Money creation can postpone correction, but it cannot abolish scarcity. It can only spread the error across more balance sheets and more years.

The deeper damage is subtler and more lasting. Easy money rewards debt over saving, consumption over patience, and leverage over disciplined capital accumulation. Households learn to live off refinancing instead of working toward durable wealth, and businesses learn to depend on cheap credit instead of genuine profitability. Government money therefore weakens the very habits that make growth sustainable. It does not produce prosperity; it produces the illusion of prosperity, followed by a bill that arrives when the illusion runs out.
