# Why Government Money Ruins Us Economically

## Chapter Digest

Government money is portrayed as destructive not only because it loses value, but because it distorts economic calculation at every level. When money can be expanded politically, prices cease to reflect underlying scarcity with the same clarity. Investment, savings, and production decisions are then made against signals that have already been tampered with. The economy does not merely become more expensive; it becomes less intelligible.

This distortion has cumulative effects. Cheap credit and expanding money supplies encourage projects that appear sustainable only because the monetary environment has been falsified. Consumption is favored over saving, debt over prudence, and political management over market coordination. The apparent short-term benefits of easy money conceal deeper fragility and misallocation.

## Key Takeaways

- Government money distorts economic calculation as well as purchasing power.
- Politically expandable money weakens price signals.
- Cheap credit encourages unsustainable projects and allocations.
- Easy money favors debt, consumption, and intervention over saving and coordination.
- Monetary manipulation creates fragility that demands still more manipulation.
