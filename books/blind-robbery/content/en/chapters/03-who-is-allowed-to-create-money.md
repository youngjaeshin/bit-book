# Who Is Allowed to Create Money and Who Is Not

## Chapter Digest

The chapter centers on an asymmetry that is usually hidden in plain sight. If an ordinary citizen prints money, the act is treated as fraud. When banks and central banks create money through credit expansion or balance-sheet operations, it is presented as normal monetary management. The question is not rhetorical: why are some institutions granted a privilege that would be criminal for everyone else?

That privilege is explained as the core mechanism of the modern system. Commercial banks create money through lending, while central banks support and coordinate the process from above. This means money creation is no longer tied to prior saving but to institutional permission. Credit expansion therefore becomes a legal pathway for selected actors to obtain purchasing power before the rest of society adjusts to the consequences.

The chapter’s force lies in its moral inversion. A system that punishes counterfeiters while granting equivalent powers to privileged institutions is not a neutral order of law but a stratified regime of access. Money creation is shown to be a political privilege, and understanding who holds it is a precondition for understanding who benefits from modern finance.

## Key Takeaways

- Modern money creation is treated as a privilege reserved for specific institutions.
- Bank credit expansion functions as legalized money creation.
- Money can be created without prior saving under the current system.
- Early recipients gain purchasing power before broader adjustment occurs.
- Monetary privilege is framed as a legal and political asymmetry.
