# Economics 1: Scarcity ~ Schelling Point

## Chapter Digest

Scarcity is the starting point because money only matters when resources are limited and choices have consequences. Once demand for a good outpaces new supply, competition appears in price, and the market begins to reward whatever can preserve value most reliably. Money is special because its scarcity gives people a reason to create abundance elsewhere; it lets them defer consumption and turn saved purchasing power into productive capital. Bitcoin enters as the first irreproducible digital unit that can be independently verified, so the old idea of scarcity finally becomes native to digital space.

That move reshapes the idea of monetary premium. Whenever the monetary function of an asset exceeds what its practical use would justify, the market is admitting that the current money is failing at storing value. In the gap before a superior money takes over, scarce assets absorb that excess demand. Bitcoin's terminal supply and decentralized consensus turn the premium into something different: not a temporary escape hatch, but a candidate for the new monetary baseline.

Gresham's Law and Thiers' Law explain what happens when weaker money and stronger money circulate together. People spend the money they expect to lose value and save the money they trust, so higher-quality money is pushed out of circulation by legal coercion or by simple rational preference. The same pattern that once described clipped coins and debased silver now describes fiat units and bitcoin, with the stronger unit increasingly becoming the one people keep.

Cantillon effects show why the path of new money matters as much as its quantity. Fresh issuance does not lift every price evenly; it enriches the earliest recipients, shifts relative prices, and quietly redistributes purchasing power. Once that distortion is visible, bitcoin looks less like just another asset and more like a Schelling point for saving and exchange, because in a digital environment people converge on the unit that best preserves value, communicates price, and resists dilution.
