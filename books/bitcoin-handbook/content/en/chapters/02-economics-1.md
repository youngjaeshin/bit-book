# Economics 1: Scarcity ~ Schelling Point

## Chapter Digest

Scarcity is the foundation because money only becomes meaningful when resources are limited and choices have consequences. The argument starts with the idea that scarce goods attract competition, and that competition shows up in price. When a good is especially good at preserving value, it acquires a monetary premium, because people are willing to pay for its monetary function as well as its practical use.

Bitcoin enters as the first irreproducible digital good that can be independently verified. That makes it absolutely scarce in a way software never had been before, and absolute scarcity changes the comparison set for money itself. From that premise, Gresham’s Law and Thiers’ Law show how weaker money is spent and stronger money is saved once multiple forms of money circulate side by side or legal tender enforcement starts to lose credibility.

The path of new money matters just as much as its supply. Cantillon effects explain why newly issued units do not raise all prices evenly, but instead benefit early recipients and distort the whole structure of relative prices. Once those distortions are visible, Bitcoin looks less like one more asset and more like a new coordination point. In a digital environment, people converge on the money that best communicates value and best preserves it, so Bitcoin begins to act like the default Schelling point for savings and exchange.
