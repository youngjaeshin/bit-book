# Technology 2: Accelerating Returns ~ Relativity

## Chapter Digest

Accelerating returns means Bitcoin has to be read inside a broader curve of technological compounding. Society has already absorbed huge shifts in shopping, media, transport, and communication, so money is not exempt from the same tempo of change. The mistake is assuming a monetary order is stable simply because it has lasted long enough to feel ordinary.

Higher-order effects are what make the transition difficult to predict. Bitcoin changes incentives, those incentives change behavior, and the changed behavior changes the environment again. Price, adoption, mining, liquidity, and confidence become linked in feedback loops, so a movement in one variable can amplify through the rest of the system instead of staying local. Reflexivity captures that recursive relationship: sentiment affects price, price affects sentiment, and the market then reacts to the reaction.

Creative destruction gives the economic meaning of that recursion. New models do not merely compete with old ones; they make the old arrangements obsolete by changing what people expect from the system. Bitcoin does that to money by dematerializing value storage and settlement, just as earlier general-purpose technologies dematerialized industries that once seemed permanent.

Relativity pulls the entire section back into monetary history. Money has always been judged against other money, and most durable monies have required real cost to acquire or defend. Against that backdrop, fiat looks less like a natural endpoint and more like a brief distortion in a much older story, while Bitcoin emerges as a harder standard that may fit the accelerating pace of technological change better than its predecessors.
