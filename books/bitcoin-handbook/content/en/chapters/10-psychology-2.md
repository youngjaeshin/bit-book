# Psychology 2: First Conclusion Bias ~ Amara’s Law

## Chapter Digest

First conclusion bias captures a common Bitcoin mistake: people grab the first explanation that feels familiar and then stop looking. New money tends to be judged by old categories, so digital scarcity gets dismissed as a fad, a scam, or a strange asset class before its properties are even examined. The safer move is to treat the first reaction as provisional and keep testing the premises that produced it.

The circle of competence and the Dunning-Kruger effect sharpen that warning. Bitcoin crosses cryptography, monetary history, thermodynamics, hardware, software, and incentives, so narrow expertise can create false confidence rather than clarity. Knowing the boundary of one's understanding is more important than pretending to see the whole system at once, and that boundary can be expanded only by disciplined study.

Category error shows why so many criticisms miss the target. Bitcoin is not a stock, a startup, or a payment app, so judging it by dividend yield, quarterly earnings, or short-term volatility mistakes one feature for the whole. The right frame has to hold several dimensions at once: monetary function, network structure, settlement assurance, and long-term savings behavior.

Gartner's hype cycle and Amara's Law explain why the social reaction remains unstable. Early excitement tends to overshoot, then disappointment follows, but genuine breakthroughs rarely move in a straight line and often need years of experimentation before they become intuitive. Bitcoin fits that pattern, so patience and curiosity are better guides than either naive enthusiasm or impatient dismissal.
