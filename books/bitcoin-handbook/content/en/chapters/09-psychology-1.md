# Psychology 1: Prisoner’s Dilemma ~ Mimetic Theory

## Chapter Digest

Bitcoin's adoption is shaped by strategy before it is shaped by sentiment. The prisoner's dilemma explains why sovereigns cannot just ignore a globally mobile monetary alternative: banning it, allowing it, regulating it, or holding it all carry different payoffs once other countries begin acting. As soon as prohibition proves ineffective, the rational choice is to reduce friction, keep capital close, and avoid watching talent and savings leave for better jurisdictions.

Jurisdictional arbitrage turns that game into a real migration path. Skilled people and mobile capital can move toward places with lower taxes, more credible money, and fewer restrictions, which weakens the old assumption that states can keep extractive policies in place without consequence. Bitcoin intensifies that leverage because it makes value portable, difficult to confiscate, and easier to protect from monetary dilution.

Social proof explains why the next wave arrives after the first wave of doubt. New monetary technology looks strange until respected investors, institutions, and public figures begin to signal that the risk is manageable. Those signals lower career risk for everyone behind them, so the crowd that once hesitated begins to move when it sees enough credible peers already committed.

Mimetic theory adds the deeper psychological layer. People imitate what appears worth wanting, often without being able to justify the desire in first principles, and Bitcoin benefits from that same social mechanism. Once a few models treat it as serious money, imitation becomes a force of its own, and the network grows through a mix of conviction, caution, and borrowed confidence.
