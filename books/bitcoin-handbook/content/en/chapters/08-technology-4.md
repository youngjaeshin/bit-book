# Technology 4: Attack Surface + Catalysts

## Chapter Digest

Security begins with simplicity. The core protocol is strong precisely because it keeps the attack surface narrow, stays easy to scrutinize, and avoids stuffing every possible function into the base layer. That restraint is not anti-innovation; it is what makes permissionless experimentation possible on top of the foundation without endangering the foundation itself. The open-source model matters here because security can only be trusted when anyone is able to inspect the code.

The catalytic side of the argument is just as important. Bitcoin solved the double-spend problem without a trusted intermediary, but the wider effect is that it forces every other monetary system to reveal its own weaknesses by comparison. Whether or not the genesis block was a comment on post-2008 bailouts, Bitcoin clearly emerged into a world of financial distrust and turned that distrust into pressure for a new option. Once a new monetary choice exists, scarcity, durability, portability, and censorship resistance all become features people actively compare instead of assumptions they passively inherit.

That comparison is what makes Bitcoin catalytic. It does not merely sit inside the market; it changes the market around it by exposing hidden trade-offs and by forcing institutions to respond. Some of the consequences are still unknowable, but the direction is clear: the protocol’s restraint at the center and its pressure on the surrounding system work together.
