# Technology 4: Attack Surface + Catalysts

## Chapter Digest

Security starts by limiting what can be attacked. Bitcoin keeps the attack surface narrow by giving the base protocol a simple job and leaving broader experimentation to layers and applications above it. That restraint matters because every extra feature added to the core would multiply unknown interactions, while a compact design makes the system easier to audit, reason about, and defend.

Open source is part of that security model. Anyone can inspect the code, test the assumptions, and verify that the rules behave as advertised, which is exactly what a monetary system needs if it is to avoid trust in gatekeepers. The protocol can remain minimal and still support permissionless experimentation elsewhere, so innovation does not have to be purchased by weakening the foundation.

Catalysts show the wider social effect. Bitcoin solved double spending without a trusted intermediary and then entered a world already shaped by financial distrust, bailouts, and frustration with existing money. By introducing a new choice, it forces direct comparison among monetary goods and exposes the hidden trade-offs in scarcity, portability, durability, and censorship resistance. The full range of consequences is still unknowable, but the direction is unmistakable: the protocol is altering the environment around it as much as it is serving the users inside it.
