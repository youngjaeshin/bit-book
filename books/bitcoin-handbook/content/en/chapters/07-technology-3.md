# Technology 3: Thermodynamics ~ Gall’s Law

## Chapter Digest

Thermodynamics grounds Bitcoin in physical reality. Proof of work converts electricity into hashes, heat, and irreversible settlement history, so block production is never free in the abstract and never detached from the world of energy. That makes Bitcoin the first rivalrous digital commodity in a serious sense: its issuance is tied to expended work, not to political permission or a promise from a central issuer.

The second law tightens that picture by making time directional. As blocks accumulate, the history of the chain becomes harder to rewrite, and the system steadily turns past work into present security. Information theory helps explain why a distributed network can still preserve a coherent ledger: one honest node can help reconstruct the state of the chain, and redundancy makes the system resilient without central control.

Moore's Law and antifragility widen the lens beyond mining. Falling hardware costs make verification and node operation more accessible over time, while distributed systems often become stronger when they are attacked, tested, or stressed. Bitcoin benefits from that kind of pressure because the protocol is conservative enough to survive shocks yet open enough for improvements on the edges.

Gall’s Law closes the chapter with a design warning. Complex systems that work usually grow from simple systems that already work, not from grand blueprints packed with every possible feature. Bitcoin's restraint is therefore not a limitation but a condition of robustness: the base layer stays narrow, the proof remains inspectable, and the architecture can support new layers without sacrificing the integrity of the foundation.
