# Technology 3: Thermodynamics ~ Gall’s Law

## Chapter Digest

Bitcoin is tied to physical reality first through thermodynamics. Proof of work converts electricity into hashes, heat, and irreversible settlement history, so issuing or reorganizing blocks is never free in the abstract. That is why the first law matters: the network depends on real energy conversion, and it rewards valid work instead of political permission. Bitcoin is presented as the first rivalrous digital commodity because its issuance is anchored to expenditure in the physical world rather than to a central promise.

The second law deepens that picture by making time one-way. As blocks accumulate, entropy and information combine to lock in the past more firmly, and the protocol’s difficulty adjustment keeps the block clock steady regardless of fluctuating hash power. Information theory and network redundancy then show why the chain survives without a central authority: one honest peer can help reconstruct and validate the whole history, which is exactly the kind of resilience a monetary network needs.

Moore’s Law and antifragility broaden the technology story beyond the chain itself. Falling hardware costs make it more realistic for ordinary people to run nodes and verify the system independently, while a distributed peer-to-peer architecture gets stronger as it is tested under stress. Gall’s Law closes the section with a design warning: complex systems that work usually grow from simple working systems, not from grand blueprints overloaded with features. Bitcoin’s slow, conservative evolution is therefore a strength, not a weakness, because the foundation has to remain intact before anything larger can be built on top of it.
