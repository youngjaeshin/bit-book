# Economics 2: Opportunity Cost ~ Jevons Paradox

## Chapter Digest

Opportunity cost becomes concrete when money itself can appreciate. Every purchase now carries the cost of the future bitcoin balance that had to be sacrificed to make it, so consumption stops looking free and saving stops looking like a passive leftover. The logic is especially sharp under hard money: if purchasing power can rise over time, the question is no longer whether something can be bought, but whether it is worth surrendering the optionality that saved capital would have created.

Time preference sits inside that same choice. A monetary system that leaks value pushes people toward immediacy, shallow horizons, and short-term survival games, while a harder monetary standard rewards patience, planning, and capital formation. The point is not that Bitcoin changes human nature; it changes the incentives around human nature, so the balance between present use and future benefit shifts in favor of delayed gratification and productive investment.

The impossible trinity pulls the argument out to the level of states. Free capital flows, fixed exchange rates, and independent monetary policy cannot all be preserved at once, so every central bank hides a trade-off and every sovereign currency inherits a limitation. Bitcoin pressures that arrangement because perfectly mobile capital can move outside the old bargain, making policy control harder to maintain and making borderless savings easier to protect.

Jevons Paradox closes the section by connecting money to energy. Efficiency gains do not necessarily reduce consumption; they often expand it, because lower costs unlock more use. Proof of work fits that dynamic rather than violating it: Bitcoin turns energy into monetary security, and by doing so it channels human ingenuity toward a system where more efficient energy use can strengthen the network instead of being absorbed by monetary dilution.
