# Economics 2: Opportunity Cost ~ Jevons Paradox

## Chapter Digest

Once money can reliably hold or gain purchasing power, every spending decision becomes a choice against the future. Opportunity cost is no longer an abstract classroom term; it becomes the price of giving up a larger balance later in order to consume now. That shifts the whole tone of economic life, because hard money forces people to ask whether a present purchase is really worth the future optionality it destroys.

Time preference runs through that logic. Inflationary money rewards immediacy, weakens saving, and pushes people toward short-term behavior because waiting erodes value. Bitcoin reverses that pressure by making patience, saving, and capital formation more rational. The result is not a magical change in human nature, but a different incentive environment that stops punishing long horizons and starts rewarding them.

The macro layer makes the same point in state policy. The impossible trinity shows that free capital flows, fixed exchange rates, and independent monetary policy cannot all be held at once, so central planners always conceal a trade-off somewhere. Bitcoin brings that trade-off back into view by offering perfectly mobile capital outside the inflationary bargain. Jevons Paradox then connects money to energy: efficiency does not reduce use, it often expands it, which is why proof of work fits so well. Bitcoin turns energy into monetary security, and in doing so it aligns incentives with innovation instead of monetary dilution.
