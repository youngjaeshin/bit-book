# Economics 3: Power Laws ~ Ansoff Matrix

## Chapter Digest

Power laws explain why bitcoin-related outcomes are so uneven. Small advantages compound into very large results, so mining concentration, wallet sales, and address balances all drift toward long tails rather than neat averages. That same shape warns against reading Bitcoin as a flat market of identical actors; once networks begin to matter, tiny differences in adoption or credibility can produce massive differences in outcome.

Winner-takes-all dynamics follow naturally from that distribution. Money behaves like a network good, and networks reward the unit that most people expect everyone else to use. Unit bias distorts that judgment by making the price of one whole coin look like the relevant comparison, when the real question is market capitalization, divisibility, and the share of a much larger monetary network that one unit represents. Bitcoin's denomination can look intimidating only when it is read at the wrong scale.

Veblen goods add a social layer to the same phenomenon. Rising price can itself strengthen demand when status and signaling matter, but Bitcoin's deeper appeal is still scarcity and liquidity, not prestige. The more important risk sits on the other side of the trade: distorted money creates distorted investment, and malinvestment piles capital into projects that only look sound because the price signals were falsified. In that world, Bitcoin's asymmetry stands out because the downside can remain bounded while the monetary upside is tied to a very large right tail.

The Ansoff lens broadens the growth picture. Bitcoin can expand by reaching new users, by adding new uses, by deepening existing holdings, and by extending into adjacent layers such as payments and savings infrastructure. The result is a monetary asset whose strategic options do not fit a single category; it behaves like a networked store of value that can grow along several paths at once.
