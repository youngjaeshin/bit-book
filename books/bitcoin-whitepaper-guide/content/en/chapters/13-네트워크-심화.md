# 5. Network — Deep Dive

## Chapter Digest

Bitcoin's network is a mesh topology. Bus, ring, star, and tree networks all fail if their central cable, hub, or root node is disrupted. In a mesh network each node connects independently to multiple others, so removing any single node leaves the network intact. Bitcoin's full nodes are scattered across countries with opposing ideologies and legal systems. Eliminating Bitcoin entirely would require taking down all approximately twenty thousand full nodes simultaneously. Blockstream operates full nodes aboard five satellites, and SpaceX's Starlink constellation of more than six thousand satellites extends global internet access to the farthest corners of the earth. Even if all nodes except one were destroyed, people would reconnect to that surviving node and redistribute the ledger. As cryptographer Ralph Merkle declared, Bitcoin is indestructible.

Bitcoin does not support or oppose any particular political system. As of 2024, the United States holds the most bitcoin of any nation, followed by China; North Korea is also estimated to hold a significant amount. In a liberal democracy Bitcoin empowers citizens to make independent economic decisions; in an authoritarian state its leaders may use it for themselves while restricting access for ordinary citizens. Any person of any belief can use Bitcoin, and no one can prevent them. This is Bitcoin's neutrality.

Bitcoin's monetary policy is determined through democratic procedures. Anyone can submit a Bitcoin Improvement Proposal (BIP), and the community deliberates before deciding whether to adopt it. BIPs are classified as Standards Track (technical changes to the consensus rules), Informational, or Process. Standards Track BIPs receive the most scrutiny because they directly alter how the protocol operates. A BIP passes through states including Draft, Proposed, Active, Final, Rejected, Withdrawn, Replaced, and Obsolete.

Miners vote on protocol changes through version bits signaling. The block header's version field is four bytes, or thirty-two bits. Three bits are reserved for future use, leaving twenty-nine bits for voting. Every 2,016 blocks miners can simultaneously signal their position on up to twenty-nine distinct proposals by setting the corresponding bits. Activating a consensus rule change requires ninety-five percent agreement — at least 1,916 of 2,016 blocks signaling support. Miner democracy is a form of representative democracy: individual miners delegate their vote to a mining pool operator, but they can switch pools instantly if the operator votes against their preference. Crucially, proof of work itself is a lottery: even a pool holding sixty percent of hash rate wins each individual block only sixty percent of the time. This mirrors the sortition practices of ancient Athens — where five hundred council members were chosen by lottery — preventing any single group from controlling every block in succession.

Full nodes hold the ultimate authority over Bitcoin's monetary policy. While only 28 mining nodes successfully mined a block in 2024, there are approximately twenty thousand full nodes on the main network. If the majority of full nodes refuse to relay a block, that block cannot propagate through the network. This mechanism is called a User-Activated Soft Fork (UASF): full nodes can reject blocks that violate rules the broader community considers important, overriding miners who might otherwise capture the protocol for their own benefit. The balance between miner democracy and full-node democracy ensures real decentralization in practice.

Economist Daron Acemoglu, winner of the 2024 Nobel Prize in Economics, demonstrated that inclusive political and economic institutions drive national prosperity, while extractive institutions lead to failure. The most vivid illustration is the Korean Peninsula: South Korea and North Korea began from the same land and the same material conditions, yet diverged radically within seventy years because they chose opposite systems. Bitcoin's consensus process encodes these democratic principles in code.

Democracy also has a critical weakness: a leader who gains power through elections can use that power to dismantle democratic institutions. Julius Caesar was elected to a republican magistracy and became dictator for life. Adolf Hitler won the German chancellorship in 1933 and used the Enabling Act to seize legislative power, establishing a one-party state. When electoral cycles are long, citizens have little recourse against a representative who betrays them. Ancient Athens addressed this through sortition; medieval Venice combined elections and lotteries in an elaborate process for selecting its Doge. Bitcoin combines representative democracy — choosing mining pools — with sortition — the random outcome of each proof-of-work race — to ensure no single party can permanently dominate the protocol.
