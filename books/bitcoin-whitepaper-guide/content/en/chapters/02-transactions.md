# Transactions

## Chapter Digest

The transactions section defines the coin not as an object but as a chain of digital signatures. Ownership is transferred when each holder signs the relevant data over to the next, creating a recordable chain. The problem is that such a chain alone cannot prevent double-spending unless some mechanism exists for agreeing which spend came first.

The commentary uses this section to show both the elegance and limitation of signature-based money. Digital signatures are enough to establish authorization, but not enough to establish uniqueness in a decentralized setting. The importance of the transaction section lies in showing that Bitcoin begins from a simple definition and immediately runs into the central challenge of digital cash: preventing one unit from being spent twice without relying on a trusted authority.

## Key Takeaways

- A coin is defined as a chain of digital signatures.
- Signatures prove authorization, not uniqueness.
- Double-spending is the first major obstacle for digital cash.
- Bitcoin starts from a simple transactional model and then builds outward.
- The section explains why a decentralized sequencing mechanism is necessary.
