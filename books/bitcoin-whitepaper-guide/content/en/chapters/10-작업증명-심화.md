# 4. Proof of Work — Deep Dive

## Chapter Digest

Proof of work consumes energy and time — and this is not merely a technical detail. It connects directly to the nature of sound money. Physics shows that the only truly scarce resources in the universe are energy and time. The First Law of Thermodynamics tells us that the total amount of energy is conserved: it neither appears from nothing nor vanishes. The Second Law tells us that entropy always increases, so energy cannot be used without limit and time flows in only one direction. These two laws are the most unshakeable truths in all of physics. Therefore, a currency whose production cost is energy and time carries scarcity that is guaranteed anywhere in the universe.

Fiat money is produced from paper and legislation. Its production cost is negligible and central authorities can issue more at will. Gold coins concentrate energy extracted through mining and refining into an economic value that resists decay, since gold does not corrode. Yet as humanity's available energy grows, gold production can expand. Before the Industrial Revolution world annual gold output was barely ten tonnes; today it exceeds three thousand tonnes. Bitcoin, by contrast, has a total supply permanently capped at 21 million coins. No matter how much human energy capacity increases, the supply cannot grow. Bitcoin is the definitive embodiment of a currency produced through energy and time.

The misconception that Bitcoin harms the environment is widespread, spread mainly by altcoin creators, altcoin investors, and legacy financial institutions. The reality is the opposite. Renewable energy sources — wind, solar, hydro — produce electricity when natural conditions allow, not when humans want them to. Generation facilities must be sized for peak demand, meaning enormous quantities of electricity are wasted as heat whenever demand is below peak. Bitcoin mining absorbs this stranded surplus. A mining rig requires only electricity and can operate in a remote hydroelectric site or harvest wind power that would otherwise be curtailed. This raises the profitability of renewable generators, incentivizing more renewable capacity. Bitcoin accelerates the transition to renewable energy.

The economic structure of mining also deserves attention. With difficulty so high today, a solo miner's chance of winning a block reward is comparable to winning the lottery. Miners must pay electricity bills continuously yet cannot predict when they will win. Mining pools emerged to solve this. In a pool many miners combine their hash power and share rewards in proportion to each miner's contribution — exactly like a lottery syndicate. Romanian economist Stefan Mandel illustrated this principle by assembling a group to purchase every lottery combination when the jackpot made expected value positive, winning fourteen times before regulations changed.

Mining pool operators cast version-bit signaling votes on behalf of their participating miners, making miner democracy a form of representative democracy. Individual miners choose a pool whose stance matches theirs, and can switch pools immediately if the pool votes differently from their preference. No pool permits itself to exceed forty percent of total hash rate, because losing miners would exodus and destroy the pool's revenue. Proof of work itself is a lottery, so even a pool with sixty percent of hash power controls only a sixty percent probability of finding each block — not every block unconditionally. This mirrors the sortition democracy of ancient Athens, preventing permanent concentration of power.

The claim that Bitcoin's price is derived from its energy cost is also worth examining. While energy does enter the production of bitcoin, energy expenditure does not by itself determine price. Price is set by supply and demand. The argument that miners' reluctance to sell below cost — driven by sunk-cost fallacy and loss-aversion bias — sustains Bitcoin's price is a purely supply-side account that reverses cause and effect. "Miner capitulation" events, where miners sell at a loss because sustained demand is insufficient, disprove this theory. Understanding Bitcoin's price properly requires examining monetary demand: its capacity to store value, to serve as a medium of exchange, and to act as a unit of account. Bitcoin's fixed supply makes it a superior store of value; its network enables value transfer at the speed of light to the opposite side of the Earth; and a currency with a fixed supply measures the prices of goods without distortion caused by monetary inflation. Bitcoin is the first and only digital currency capable of fulfilling all three monetary functions simultaneously.
