# Timestamp Server

## Chapter Digest

The timestamp server section explains how ordering is secured. By hashing transactions into an ongoing chain and publishing proof that this ordering existed at a given moment, Bitcoin creates a public record that is costly to alter. The issue is not wall-clock time in an ordinary sense, but a reliable sequence of events that all participants can audit.

The commentary stresses that this is where the “chain of blocks” logic begins to matter. Hashes, chaining, and the one-way nature of cryptographic functions make the record tamper-resistant. The timestamp server is therefore not a cosmetic appendage to the transaction model; it is the mechanism that begins converting digital signatures into a shared and defensible history.

## Key Takeaways

- The timestamp server secures the order of transactions.
- Bitcoin needs sequence, not merely signatures.
- Hash-chaining makes the record costly to alter.
- Publicly auditable ordering replaces private trust.
- This section is the first real step toward a blockchain logic.
