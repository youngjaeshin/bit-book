# Introduction — Intuitive Understanding

## Chapter Digest

Online commerce has relied almost entirely on trusted third parties (TTPs) such as financial institutions to process electronic payments. If transactions cannot be reversed, sellers can defraud buyers; if they can be reversed, buyers can defraud sellers. To resolve this dilemma, intermediaries like card companies and platforms step in, but their involvement generates four structural costs: mediation fees, excessive personal information demands, minimum transaction thresholds that block micropayments, and settlement delays that starve small merchants of working capital.

Buying a fish-shaped pastry with cash requires no name, no credit score, no home address. Yet the moment the same transaction moves online, a third party intervenes, fees attach, and personal data must be surrendered. Even irreversible services — a six-hour hair extension, a restaurant meal already consumed — become reversible under the chargeback system, leaving sellers to absorb the loss. When every transaction mandates a mediator, the mediator accumulates outsized power, and the cost is ultimately borne by both parties.

Satoshi Nakamoto proposed a peer-to-peer electronic payment system grounded in cryptographic proof rather than trust. In Bitcoin, once a transaction is made it cannot be undone; refunds are handled not by canceling the original transaction but by the seller sending a new payment back to the buyer — exactly as cash works. Where mediation is genuinely needed, such as shipped goods, an escrow mechanism can be layered on optionally, but the default is direct party-to-party settlement. For this system to function, the double-spend problem must be solved, and Bitcoin addresses it through a distributed timestamp server.
