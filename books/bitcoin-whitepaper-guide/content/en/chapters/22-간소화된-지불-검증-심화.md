# Chapter 8 Deep Understanding — Rationalism and Bitcoin

The core message of Simplified Payment Verification is that verification replaces trust. A light node can verify data on the network without fully trusting any third party, which aligns with Bitcoin's foundational philosophy: "Don't trust, verify." This motto is a deliberate rewrite of the Russian proverb "Trust, but verify," popularized by Ronald Reagan.

Humanity has advanced along two great axes: trust and verification. Their extreme expressions are religion and science. Religion seeks truth through belief and produces centralized power structures. As Yuval Harari argues, homo sapiens dominated the earth partly because shared beliefs allowed strangers to cooperate and organize beyond the Dunbar number of roughly 150. Religion, nations, sports teams, and corporations are all shared fictions that enable strangers to trust one another and divide labor. Yet trust always carries the risk of betrayal; evolutionary psychology suggests that a certain proportion of defectors is unavoidable, meaning every trust-based collective tolerates some level of fraud.

Science takes the opposite approach. Modern science was born from the rationalist movement — the pursuit of truth through doubt and verification rather than belief. Rationalism traces back to Plato and Aristotle, was revived by René Descartes, who insisted on doubting all prior beliefs to find certain truth, and was extended by Francis Bacon, who argued that truth is approached through empirical experiment. Karl Popper completed scientific rationalism by asserting that only falsifiable claims qualify as scientific; anything that cannot in principle be proven wrong is pseudoscience. Since only what is verifiable can be trusted, rationalism is fundamentally about distributing the power to verify.

Bitcoin resembles science — rationalism — rather than religion. Anyone who wants to know whether Bitcoin's supply is truly capped at 21 million, whether there was no pre-mine, or whether the genesis block's reward is unspendable, can check the code directly. This is the significance of open source. More doubt and more verification make Bitcoin more trustworthy.

Satoshi Nakamoto's disappearance is best understood in this light. Had Satoshi remained, human instinct would have elevated them to an authority figure, and centralization would have followed. By removing themselves from the system entirely, Satoshi enabled genuine decentralization. If Satoshi returned today and wanted to change the code, they would have to propose the change, accept criticism, debate it, and convince the community — exactly like anyone else.

Bitcoin's community is deeply conservative about decentralization values, which also stems from rationalism. The block size war and hard fork debates demonstrate the community's refusal to compromise on the ability for anyone to participate in verification. Separating the mining function from the full-node function serves the same purpose: mining must become progressively harder for the system to remain stable, but transaction verification must remain accessible to everyone.

Bitcoiners extend this skepticism even to their wallets. Some run full nodes and connect their wallet apps to their own node to avoid trusting a third party's balance display. Others use air-gapped hardware wallets to minimize trust in the device manufacturer, and cross-check address generation rules on independent sites. Each individual act of self-verification contributes integrity and robustness to the Bitcoin network.

Full nodes receive no financial incentive, yet people run them for the stability of the ecosystem, for security, and as a practice of Bitcoin's philosophy. Once one internalizes Bitcoin's philosophy, the aesthetic of doubt and verification becomes apparent. Bitcoiners trust nothing and verify everything — and so they run full nodes. "Don't trust, verify" is not merely a slogan; it is the epistemological revolution Bitcoin proposes to the world.
