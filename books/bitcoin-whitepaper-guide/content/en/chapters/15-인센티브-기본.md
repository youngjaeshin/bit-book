# 6. Incentive — Basic

Chapter 6 of the Bitcoin whitepaper explains why miners are compelled to act honestly and how bitcoin as a currency is introduced into circulation. The starting point is the concept of the coinbase transaction.

## The Coinbase Transaction: The Origin of All UTXOs

Bitcoin uses a UTXO model rather than an account balance model. Every transaction must use unspent outputs from prior transactions as its inputs. So where does the very first output come from? The answer is the coinbase transaction. By convention, the first transaction in every block is always a coinbase transaction. It references no prior transaction and instead creates new bitcoin directly into the block miner's wallet address. The coinbase transaction is the only mechanism by which new bitcoin can be produced. Anyone holding bitcoin can trace it back through a chain of transactions to some coinbase transaction.

Proof of work consumes enormous computational resources and electricity. No miner would bear these costs without compensation. Therefore, the node that succeeds at proof of work receives newly minted coins via the coinbase transaction. This is the incentive that sustains the network. Just as gold miners expend resources to expand the gold supply, bitcoin miners expend CPU time and electricity. This is why proof of work is called "mining."

## Fixed Supply and the Halving

Bitcoin's total supply is fixed at approximately 21 million BTC and cannot be altered by anyone. Any software that modifies this limit would be rejected by all nodes. The coinbase reward started at 50 BTC and is cut in half exactly every 210,000 blocks — roughly every four years, since blocks are produced on average every 10 minutes. After the genesis block in January 2009, halvings reduced the reward to 25 BTC in 2012, 12.5 BTC in 2016, 6.25 BTC in 2020, and 3.125 BTC in 2024. By the time of the fourth halving in 2024, 93.75% of the total supply had already been issued. Around 2140, the block reward will fall to zero, after which only transaction fees will incentivize miners.

When the reward halves, miner revenue drops sharply. Those for whom mining becomes unprofitable shut off their machines — a phenomenon called "miner capitulation." When total hash power falls, the difficulty adjustment lowers the mining difficulty two weeks later, restoring profitability and drawing miners back. The fixed, predictable supply schedule means that no matter how high the bitcoin price rises, the rate of new supply cannot be increased. Humanity has never before had a monetary asset or currency with a precisely fixed production rate.

## Pre-mining and Fairness

Satoshi Nakamoto mined the genesis block on January 3, 2009 and released Bitcoin to the public on January 8, 2009. From January 8 onward anyone could participate in mining. Between the genesis block and the public release, Satoshi did not mine any additional blocks — the second block was mined on January 9, the day after the release. Furthermore, the 50 BTC coinbase reward from the genesis block is permanently unspendable by design, closing off any possibility of pre-mining. Many other cryptocurrencies have no supply cap, or their creators reserved large portions for themselves and early investors before public launch — a structure that continuously transfers value from currency users to the issuers.

## Transaction Fee Incentive

When the output value of a transaction is less than its input value, the difference becomes a transaction fee paid to the miner who includes that transaction in a block. Bitcoin fees are charged per unit of transaction data size (denominated in satoshis per virtual byte, SATS/vB), not as a percentage of the transaction amount. This makes large-value transfers very cost-efficient compared to card payment systems. Fees also defend the network against denial-of-service (DoS) attacks: without fees, an attacker could flood the network with millions of trivial transactions, congesting the block space and delaying legitimate transactions.

The fee problem for small payments is solved by the Lightning Network, a second-layer protocol that processes transactions nearly instantly and at negligible cost, theoretically handling millions of transactions per second.

A transaction does not need to be confirmed in a block before a merchant accepts it. If a reasonable fee is set and the transaction has propagated through the network, the merchant can treat it as essentially certain to confirm — just as a café owner hands over coffee without waiting three business days for a card settlement.

## Incentives and Security: Honesty as the Dominant Strategy

The claim that bitcoin will collapse in 2140 when coinbase rewards disappear is wrong. The whitepaper states explicitly: once the predetermined number of coins has been released, the incentive transitions entirely to transaction fees. This is actually beneficial — as halvings reduce the coinbase share of miner revenue, the growing share from transaction fees makes the reward structure more stable and less vulnerable to post-halving security concerns.

Consider an attacker, Mallory, with massive hash power. She has two options: use that power to attempt fraud, or mine honestly and collect rewards. Broadcasting an invalid block costs her the enormous electricity bill of the proof of work while gaining nothing — every other node rejects it. If she actually succeeded in attacking the network, the resulting loss of confidence would crash the value of any bitcoin she holds. The cost of attacking is astronomical: calculations based on the U.S. GDP suggest that even the U.S. government would need to devote the entire productive output of its citizens every ten minutes just to have a chance of successfully rewriting a single block. In practice, the world's two largest bitcoin-holding governments — the U.S. and China — have every incentive to protect the network, not attack it. Any entity with meaningful hash power finds that honest mining produces stable income with no risk, whereas attacking produces near-certain loss at immense expense. Honesty is the rational dominant strategy.
