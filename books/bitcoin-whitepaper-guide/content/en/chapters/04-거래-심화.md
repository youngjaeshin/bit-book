# 2. Transactions — Deep Dive

## Chapter Digest

Creating a Bitcoin wallet requires no identification, no bank visit, and no registration with any authority. All you do is pick a number — any 256-bit number. That number becomes your private key. The total number of possible 256-bit values is approximately 1 followed by 77 zeros, a figure so vast it exceeds human comprehension. After choosing that number, you tell no one. There is no institution to notify. The only rule is that you must never reveal it.

A natural concern arises: could someone else pick the same number? This is where randomness matters. Humans love patterns, and when left to choose numbers themselves they unconsciously introduce patterns that reduce entropy. Using a phone number as a private key, for example, shrinks the possibility space to about 100 million values — easily swept by a laptop in under a second through a brute-force attack. A specialized ASIC machine available in 2024 for roughly the price of a mid-range car can perform 200 trillion operations per second. Computer random-number generators also carry risk: they produce pseudorandom numbers seeded from initial values rather than true randomness. In 2013, an Android wallet vulnerability caused a nonce to be reused during signing, allowing attackers to solve simultaneous equations and recover the actual private key, draining the affected wallets.

The safest method for generating a private key is physically flipping a coin 256 times or rolling a die more than 100 times. Claude Shannon's information entropy provides the mathematical framework here. A properly generated private key has 256 bits of entropy. At that level, converting the entire Milky Way galaxy into a computer and running it for the age of the universe — 13.8 billion years — would allow it to scan only about 0.0001432 percent of all possible keys, and that estimate ignores the energy required for each computation. Bruce Schneier wrote that a brute-force attack on a 256-bit key is computationally infeasible regardless of advances in technology, because the thermodynamic limits of the universe cannot be overcome. In contrast, using a grammatically coherent English phrase as a private key reduces entropy to roughly 35.2 bits, which is trivially within reach of modern supercomputers.

Bitcoin ownership is private-key ownership. Whoever knows the private key controls the coins associated with the corresponding address. If the private key is lost, ownership cannot be claimed. James Howells of the UK accidentally threw away a hard drive containing the private keys to 8,000 BTC. Every full node on the Bitcoin network still holds the record showing those coins exist at that address, but without the private key Howells cannot touch them. He has spent years petitioning his local council to let him excavate the landfill site, so far without success.

Bitcoin's lack of physical form makes it uniquely resistant to seizure. American senators once proposed a law requiring disclosure of bitcoin holdings at the border, not realizing that private keys can be memorized and carried invisibly in one's mind. During the Russia-Ukraine war, refugees memorized their mnemonic seed phrases, crossed into other countries, and withdrew cash from Bitcoin ATMs abroad. No border patrol can confiscate what exists only as memory.

Bitcoin wallets do not hold bitcoin — they hold private keys. There are two broad categories: custodial wallets, where a third party holds the keys, and self-custodial wallets, where you hold the keys yourself. Exchanges are custodial wallets. When you leave bitcoin on an exchange, you do not control the private key; the exchange does. Your "ownership" is really just a claim that you trust the exchange to honor. The 2014 Mt. Gox hack, which at the time handled over 80 percent of global Bitcoin trading volume, wiped out roughly 650,000 BTC. South Korean exchanges Bithumb and Upbit suffered major breaches in subsequent years. These attacks succeed because exchanges store large amounts of bitcoin in hot wallets — devices connected to the internet.

Self-custodial wallets split into hot wallets, which are internet-connected, and cold wallets, which are not. Traditional cold wallets required briefly connecting to a networked PC to broadcast a signed transaction, momentarily recreating hot-wallet conditions. Air-gapped wallets eliminate this exposure entirely: they produce signed transactions as QR codes that can be scanned by a networked device without the signing device ever touching the internet, Bluetooth, or USB. For long-term storage of significant amounts, an air-gapped cold wallet is the safest option available.

DeFi platforms and altcoins carry the same systemic risks as the legacy financial system. Smart contracts can have exploitable bugs; operating entities create moral-hazard problems; and the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission has classified 68 altcoins — including Ripple, BNB, Solana, and Cardano — as unregistered securities on the grounds that their value depends on the efforts of an identifiable issuing entity. The SEC has explicitly stated that Bitcoin is not a security, because it has no operating company and no one whose efforts determine its value.

Most modern Bitcoin wallets use the HD (Hierarchical Deterministic) standard. The process starts with a sufficiently random entropy number, which is converted into a mnemonic — a list of 12 or 24 English words. The words are not the source of entropy; they are a human-readable encoding of the entropy that was generated first. From this single mnemonic, an unlimited number of private keys can be derived deterministically. The mnemonic must be stored entirely offline. Writing it in a memo app, photographing it, or saving it to any cloud-synced storage is equivalent to leaving the key to a vault in a public square. The core principle is simple: not your keys, not your bitcoin.
