# 11. Calculation — Deep Understanding

## The Byzantine Generals Problem and the 51% Attack

### The Two Generals Problem

Before examining the Byzantine Generals Problem, it helps to understand the Two Generals Problem, proposed by Jim Gray in 1978. Two generals — Alice and Bob — command armies besieging a city from the south and north. They can only win by attacking simultaneously. However, any messenger sent between them might be captured. Alice cannot be certain Bob received her proposed attack time, and even if Bob acknowledges receipt, Alice cannot be certain that acknowledgment arrived. Every message requires another confirming reply, producing an infinite regress that is mathematically unsolvable. Even TCP/IP, the foundation of the modern internet, does not fully solve this — it settles for a three-way handshake as a practical compromise.

### The Byzantine Generals Problem

The Byzantine Generals Problem, formulated in 1982 by Leslie Lamport, Robert Shostak, and Marshall Pease, extends this to multiple parties including traitors. Among four generals besieging a city, at least three must act in concert for the operation to succeed — but one general, Mallory, is a traitor who sends contradictory information to the others. Alice decides to attack and notifies the group. Carol relays accurate information; Mallory tells Bob that Alice plans to retreat. Bob now receives conflicting information and cannot determine whether the majority is attacking or retreating. This problem had been considered unsolvable for distributed networks seeking a single consensus in the presence of malicious participants.

### Bitcoin's Byzantine Fault Tolerance

Satoshi Nakamoto stated explicitly that "the proof-of-work chain is a solution to the Byzantine Generals Problem." In Bitcoin, each general holds a computer. When a general receives a message proposing an attack time, they attempt to find a proof-of-work for a block containing that message. A solution is broadcast to neighbors, who relay it further while beginning work on the next block. If two conflicting blocks appear simultaneously, the longest-chain rule eventually selects one, and all participants converge on a single history. A system that achieves consensus despite the presence of Byzantine (malicious) nodes is called **Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT)**.

Bitcoin achieves BFT as long as honest nodes hold more than 51% of total hash power. If an attacker acquires 51% or more, double-spends become possible over the long run.

### Real-World 51% Attacks on Altcoins

Bitcoin has never suffered a successful double-spend or block-withholding attack. However, altcoins with smaller total hash power have been victimized. **Bitcoin Gold** was attacked in May 2018: an attacker acquired over 51% of its hash power, secretly mined blocks redirecting funds to their own address, meanwhile sent the same coins to exchanges and converted them to dollars or bitcoin, then broadcast the longer private chain to invalidate the exchange deposits. The attacker extracted approximately $18 million. Bitcoin Gold's price fell from roughly $53 to $9 over the following months — a loss of about 83% of value.

Bitcoin Gold was attacked again in January 2022. Other altcoins that suffered 51% attacks include Monacoin (May 2018), ZenCash (June 2018), Ethereum Classic (January 2019), and Litecoin Cash (July 2019). These cases demonstrate directly that Bitcoin's enormous total hash power is not waste — it is the very source of its security. An attacker who cannot feasibly command even 1% of Bitcoin's hash power faces an astronomically low probability of success.
