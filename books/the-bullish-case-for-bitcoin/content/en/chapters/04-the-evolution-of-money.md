# The Evolution of Money

## Chapter Digest

Money does not usually appear fully formed. It moves through stages. A collectible gains demand because people like owning it. A good store of value becomes something people save precisely because they expect it to hold purchasing power. Only after that does it become a medium of exchange, and only later does it stabilize as a unit of account. Gold followed that pattern over centuries, and the process fed on itself because each step made the good more useful to the next holder.

That sequence is path dependent. Once a monetary good accumulates users, it becomes harder to dislodge, because the value of holding it depends partly on everyone else's willingness to accept it. States can interrupt that evolution with legal tender laws, taxes, capital controls, or outright prohibition, but coercion does not erase the underlying logic. The market still rewards the asset that best stores value, best moves across space, and best survives the political cycle. The path may be bent, but the selection pressure remains.

Bitcoin enters that evolutionary ladder early. First, it works as a scarce digital asset that people hold. Only later, if trust continues to deepen, does it gain broader exchange use and eventually the status of money in the fuller sense. That framing matters because it prevents the common mistake of judging Bitcoin only by how often it is spent today. Every successful money began by being held before it was spent, and Bitcoin is no exception.
