# The Evolution of Money

## Chapter Digest

Money does not arrive in its final form. A good first becomes desirable as a collectible, then gains acceptance as a store of value, later matures into a medium of exchange, and only after that settles into the role of unit of account. Much contemporary criticism of Bitcoin fails because it reverses this sequence and judges Bitcoin by whether it already performs the functions of the endpoint. The argument here insists that store-of-value adoption comes first and that medium-of-exchange utility follows only once monetary confidence has deepened and price volatility has fallen.

That framework reframes Bitcoin’s uneven market history. Violent booms and busts are not treated as proof that monetization has failed; they are treated as the normal path by which a new monetary good is discovered and repriced. New cohorts enter for different reasons. Some are drawn by ideology, some by technical understanding, some by macroeconomic stress, and some by the realization that a growing monetary network may still be far from saturation. As each wave enters, a new level of social proof forms, then gets tested by collapse.

The result is a view of Bitcoin’s price history as an intelligible process rather than chaos. Hype cycles, crashes, and renewed adoption are the mechanisms through which conviction is filtered, ownership broadens, and volatility gradually declines. What appears externally as speculative madness is recast as the historical pattern through which a superior store of value advances toward money.

## Key Takeaways

- Money matures through stages rather than appearing fully formed.
- Store of value precedes medium of exchange.
- Bitcoin should be judged by where it stands in monetary evolution, not by the end state alone.
- Monetization proceeds through repeated cycles of adoption and repricing.
- Volatility is treated as a feature of early monetization.
