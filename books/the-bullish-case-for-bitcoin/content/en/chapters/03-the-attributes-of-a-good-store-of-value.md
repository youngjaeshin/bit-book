# The Attributes of a Good Store of Value

## Chapter Digest

Stores of value do not compete merely through familiarity or official endorsement. They compete through monetary properties: durability, portability, fungibility, verifiability, divisibility, scarcity, established history, and censorship resistance. These properties determine whether wealth can be preserved across time, moved across distance, verified in trade, and protected against coercion. A good that performs better on these dimensions has a stronger claim to absorb savings from its rivals.

Gold remains formidable because it combines durability with civilizational legitimacy accumulated over centuries. Fiat currencies persist for different reasons. Their role is protected by state power, legal systems, and entrenched financial networks, even though they are weak as long-term stores of value. Bitcoin claims superiority by combining extreme portability, simple verification, high divisibility, hard scarcity, and powerful censorship resistance in a single monetary good. What gold solved physically, Bitcoin attempts to solve digitally and with lower dependence on trusted institutions.

The chief weakness in Bitcoin’s case is not one of design but one of age. Gold’s established history still matters because monetary trust has inertia. People do not abandon incumbent stores of value overnight, even when the challenger has better technical properties. The contest is therefore framed as a long struggle between stronger monetary characteristics and the accumulated legitimacy of older monetary standards.

## Key Takeaways

- Monetary competition can be judged through store-of-value attributes.
- Scarcity and censorship resistance are core monetary traits, not side issues.
- Gold’s strongest advantage is long historical legitimacy.
- Fiat survives through political power and network lock-in.
- Bitcoin’s main weakness is short history rather than weak design.
