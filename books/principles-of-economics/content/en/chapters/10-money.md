# Chapter 10: Money

## Chapter Digest

Money is treated as the most marketable good that emerges to facilitate exchange and hold purchasing power across time. What matters in money is not the number of units held, but the purchasing power those units command. The chapter therefore develops money as a market institution that coordinates exchange and reduces the frictions of barter.

Because money is demanded for its exchangeability, it has a unique role in economic calculation and coordination. The chapter rejects the idea that there can be a socially optimal amount of money produced by policy makers; what matters is the integrity and purchasing power of the monetary good. Money is thus central not because it is a state creation, but because it is the medium that connects the entire market order.

## Key Takeaways

- Money emerges as the most marketable good.
- Purchasing power matters more than the nominal count of units.
- Money solves the frictions of barter and supports calculation.
- Demand for money is tied to exchangeability and coordination.
- The integrity of money matters more than managed quantity targets.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Money is a market institution, not fundamentally a state invention.
- Purchasing power is the relevant measure for cash holdings.
- The chapter resists quantity-management views of money.
