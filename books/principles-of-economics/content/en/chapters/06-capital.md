# Chapter 6: Capital

## Chapter Digest

Capital is presented as the product of advance provision, saving, and the willingness to prepare today for needs that will arise later. Civilized life depends on long production structures in which present effort and stored resources serve future consumption. Capital therefore reflects both material accumulation and a temporal orientation that places future output above immediate use.

The chapter stresses that capital deepens production and raises living standards by extending the range and productivity of human action. A society rich in capital is one that has accumulated tools, processes, and provisions that allow more roundabout but more fruitful production. Capital accumulation is thus inseparable from time preference, planning, and the institutional conditions that protect saving.

## Key Takeaways

- Capital is the result of saving and advance provision for future needs.
- Civilized production depends on long, time-consuming structures.
- Capital deepens productivity and raises living standards.
- Roundabout production is more productive when supported by capital.
- Capital accumulation depends on planning and lower time preference.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Capital embodies future-oriented preparation.
- Production becomes more fruitful as it becomes more capital intensive.
- Saving and institutional stability are prerequisites for capital growth.
