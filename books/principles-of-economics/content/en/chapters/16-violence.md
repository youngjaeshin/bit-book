# Chapter 16: Violence

## Chapter Digest

After tracing voluntary action, exchange, and market order, the book turns to violence as the opposite principle. Violence interrupts voluntary coordination by replacing consent with coercion. The chapter therefore treats aggression not merely as a moral problem but as an economic one: it redirects action away from mutually beneficial cooperation and toward defense, loss, and destruction.

The discussion then focuses on state coercion and the rationales offered in its defense. These rationales are examined critically and found internally inconsistent or motivated by the desire to justify aggression. Violence is therefore presented as a disruptive force that must be distinguished sharply from the voluntary order analyzed in earlier chapters.

## Key Takeaways

- Violence is the opposite of voluntary market coordination.
- Aggression changes economic incentives and destroys cooperation.
- Coercion is an economic as well as moral problem.
- State violence is examined through the justifications offered for it.
- Voluntary order and aggression must be conceptually separated.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- The non-aggression principle is central in this chapter’s framing.
- Violence redirects resources from production to conflict.
- State rationales for coercion are treated skeptically.
