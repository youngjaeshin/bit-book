# Chapter 13: Time Preference

## Chapter Digest

Time preference is developed here as the rate at which individuals discount the future relative to the present. A lower time preference allows saving, capital accumulation, delayed gratification, and the extension of planning horizons, while a higher time preference compresses action toward immediate satisfaction. The chapter links this concept not only to economics, but to the broader process of civilization.

As time preference falls, people can invest more in durable structures, knowledge, cooperation, and social order. The chapter therefore presents time preference as a deep variable connecting personal choice, capital growth, and cultural development. Economic life cannot be understood fully without seeing how present and future are ranked in action.

## Key Takeaways

- Time preference measures the discounting of the future relative to the present.
- Lower time preference supports saving and capital accumulation.
- Higher time preference favors immediate satisfaction over longer plans.
- Civilization is linked to lower time preference and longer horizons.
- The concept connects personal choice with broad social outcomes.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Time preference is one of the deepest causal variables in economics.
- Saving and civilization both depend on future orientation.
- Cultural development is linked to temporal ordering in choice.
