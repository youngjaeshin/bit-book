# Chapter 17: Defense

## Chapter Digest

If violence is real and defense is necessary, the question becomes whether defense must be monopolized by the state. This chapter examines defense, law, and order as services that can be analyzed economically like other scarce goods. A monopoly of defense is not assumed to be self-justifying; it must be compared to alternative institutional arrangements and judged by its incentives and results.

The chapter argues that state monopoly in defense and law carries failure modes similar to monopoly elsewhere: weak feedback, poor incentives, coercive financing, and limited accountability. A freer market in defense is therefore explored not as a utopian fantasy, but as a serious institutional alternative to compulsory monopoly.

## Key Takeaways

- Defense and law can be analyzed as economic services.
- State monopoly is not automatically justified.
- Monopoly problems appear in defense as in other sectors.
- Incentives and accountability matter in protective services.
- A freer defense order is explored as a real alternative.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Defense is treated within economic analysis rather than outside it.
- Monopoly failure applies to law and protection too.
- Coercive financing weakens the case for state monopoly.
