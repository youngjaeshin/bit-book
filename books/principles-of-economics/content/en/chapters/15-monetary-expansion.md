# Chapter 15: Monetary Expansion

## Chapter Digest

Monetary expansion is analyzed as a process that creates claims without creating real capital. Credit expansion may appear to stimulate growth, but it cannot substitute for saved resources and genuine capital formation. The chapter follows the Austrian argument that artificially expanded credit distorts relative prices, interest rates, and production plans, setting in motion a cycle of misallocation.

What looks like prosperity in the short run is therefore treated as a false signal. Monetary expansion channels resources into lines of production that cannot be sustained once the underlying scarcity of real capital reasserts itself. The boom contains the seeds of the bust because the new structure is built on monetary illusion rather than real saving.

## Key Takeaways

- Monetary expansion does not create real capital.
- Artificial credit growth distorts prices, rates, and production plans.
- Apparent short-run prosperity can be misleading.
- Misallocation follows when monetary signals diverge from real savings.
- Boom and bust are linked through monetary expansion.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Credit cannot substitute for capital.
- Monetary expansion generates false signals in production.
- Busts correct structures built on unsustainable expansion.
