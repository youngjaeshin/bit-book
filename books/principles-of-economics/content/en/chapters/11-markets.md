# Chapter 11: Markets

## Chapter Digest

The market economy is described as the social system of division of labor under private ownership of the means of production. Each person acts on his own behalf, yet those actions are directed into serving others because exchange and production depend on satisfying the wants of others. The market is therefore not chaos but a coordinating process in which decentralized action becomes socially ordered.

Prices, competition, and entrepreneurial adjustment allow this order to emerge without central direction. The chapter treats markets as discovery mechanisms that reveal information, redirect resources, and align production with consumer demand. What appears impersonal is actually a dense web of mutual service mediated through exchange.

## Key Takeaways

- Markets coordinate the division of labor under private property.
- Self-interest is channeled into service to others through exchange.
- Prices and competition transmit information and incentives.
- Markets are discovery processes, not disorderly accidents.
- Decentralized action can generate social order without central control.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- The market economy is defined institutionally, not emotionally.
- Competition is part of coordination and discovery.
- Market order emerges from voluntary exchange under private ownership.
