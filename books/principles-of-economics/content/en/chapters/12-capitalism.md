# Chapter 12: Capitalism

## Chapter Digest

Capitalism is presented as the social order in which private property, voluntary exchange, capital accumulation, and entrepreneurial action are allowed to operate together. It is not defended as a moral slogan detached from outcomes, but as the institutional arrangement most consistent with production, cooperation, and rising living standards. Capitalism works because it allows economizing individuals to coordinate through ownership, price signals, and profit-and-loss feedback.

The chapter also treats hostility to capitalism as rooted in misunderstanding of how social wealth is generated. What appears unequal or impersonal on the surface is in fact tied to a system that rewards service, calculation, and long-term production. Capitalism is thus defended as civilization under private property rather than as a narrow ideology of greed.

## Key Takeaways

- Capitalism combines private property, voluntary exchange, and capital accumulation.
- Profit and loss provide feedback for social coordination.
- Capitalism is defended on productive and civilizational grounds.
- Critiques often misunderstand how wealth is created.
- The chapter treats capitalism as the institutional extension of market cooperation.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Capitalism is linked to civilization under private property.
- Profit-and-loss signals are central to coordination.
- The system rewards productive service rather than abstract greed.
