# Chapter 14: Credit and Banking

## Chapter Digest

Credit and banking are treated as institutions that can either coordinate saving and investment soundly or become channels of monetary distortion. Credit in its sound form transfers real resources across time by linking savers and borrowers under real constraints. Banking becomes dangerous when it ceases to be an intermediary and instead expands claims beyond the real savings available to support them.

The chapter therefore draws a sharp distinction between credit grounded in actual saving and bank practices that multiply claims and weaken monetary integrity. Because money and credit penetrate all economic relations, distortion here has system-wide consequences. Banking is not peripheral to the economy; it shapes the structure of intertemporal coordination itself.

## Key Takeaways

- Credit can either coordinate real saving and investment or distort them.
- Sound credit is grounded in actual saved resources.
- Banking becomes dangerous when claims exceed real savings.
- Money and credit affect the entire structure of economic relations.
- Distortion in banking spreads system-wide.

## Notable Terms / Claims

- Credit must be distinguished by whether it is backed by real saving.
- Banking shapes intertemporal coordination across the economy.
- Monetary integrity is threatened when claims multiply beyond resources.
