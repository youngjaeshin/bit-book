# Fiat Money I

## Chapter Digest

Fiat money survives because it solves a real coordination problem even while introducing deeper distortions. Ammous does not treat fiat as a simple fraud from the outset. It spread because state-issued money, operating through banking networks and government liabilities, became highly salable across space. It could move through large economies, settle taxes, support long-distance trade, and standardize accounting at national scale more effectively than fragmented metal-based systems.

That practical success, however, comes with a structural shift: money ceases to be a neutral asset chosen in markets and becomes a liability issued inside a political hierarchy. What circulates is no longer final settlement in commodity form but a stack of claims stabilized by state power, banking privilege, and legal obligation. Fiat therefore expands by multiplying balance-sheet promises rather than by increasing the supply of a costly underlying asset.

This is why the chapter treats fiat as an engineering trade-off rather than a simple historical mistake. The gain is salability across space and state-backed scalability. The cost is that the monetary base becomes easier to manipulate, easier to weaponize for fiscal ends, and more vulnerable to cumulative distortions once politics gains access to money creation. Fiat works, but it works by changing what money is and who controls it.

The deeper claim is that this trade-off cannot be evaluated only by looking at short-term convenience. A system that makes settlement and payment easier at scale may still impose hidden long-run costs through debt expansion, fragile credit structures, and incentives that reward proximity to the issuer. Fiat’s rise is therefore explained not as proof of superiority in every respect, but as proof that salability across space was purchased at the cost of soundness and sovereignty.
