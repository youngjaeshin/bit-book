# Fiat Money III

## Chapter Digest

The third fiat-money unit pushes from mechanics to social consequence. If fiat’s architecture allows balances to be expanded and rolled forward indefinitely, then its monetary elasticity becomes a means of control as much as coordination. Ammous emphasizes universal debt dependence: the system does not merely circulate money, it organizes economic life around debt claims, refinancing pressures, and state-backed banking privilege. In this sense fiat balances are not neutral units but instruments that tie households, firms, and states into one debt lattice.

That is why the question “what is fiat good for?” is asked polemically. Fiat is very good at financing states, rescuing politically favored institutions, and making hard constraints disappear for those closest to the spigot. It is far less good at preserving honest signals, long-term saving discipline, or broad social fairness. The chapter reads fiat’s usefulness as highly asymmetric: enormous for insiders, ambiguous or destructive for everyone else.

Bitcoin is therefore foreshadowed here as an exit from debt-mediated monetary dependence. Before the book turns fully to life under fiat, it establishes that fiat’s money layer already carries a political economy of privilege inside it. The monetary unit cannot be separated from the power structure that issues it.

## Key Takeaways

- Fiat balances are embedded in a debt-based power structure.
- Monetary elasticity enables control as well as coordination.
- Fiat’s benefits accrue unevenly, favoring those nearest issuance.
- The system weakens hard constraints for insiders while preserving them for others.
- Bitcoin is foreshadowed as an alternative to debt dependence.
